Before We Start — A Quick Foundation

You're new to unit trusts. Three short notes before we dive into the recommendations.

1. What is a Unit Trust?

A unit trust is a pool of money from many investors, professionally managed and invested across a basket of assets — stocks, bonds, cash — on your behalf. You own units; the fund manager does the work.

2. How Returns Work

Your investment grows in two ways: the price per unit (NAV) rises as the underlying assets appreciate, and some funds distribute income periodically. Reinvesting distributions compounds your growth.

3. Your Cooling-Off Right

As a first-time investor with Public Mutual, you have a 6 business day cooling-off period from the date of your first purchase. If you change your mind, you may redeem your units at the original NAV paid — no loss on the principal.

2. Distribution Policy

  • Reinvest distributionsreinvestAlways reinvest any income distributions back into the same fund — compounding extra units over 20 years meaningfully increases the final portfolio value.
  • Avoid income payoutspayoutTaking cash payouts now would interrupt compounding and reduce the number of units working for you, slowing progress toward the 7% annual growth target.
  • Review at goal milestoneShift to a payout strategy only when you are within 3–5 years of needing the money — not before, to preserve maximum growth runway.

3. Rebalancing Triggers

  • Annual reviewtime-basedCheck portfolio weights every 12 months and top up or trim funds whose allocations have drifted to restore the target split.
  • Drift banddrift-basedRebalance immediately if any fund's allocation moves more than 10 percentage points above or below its target weight, regardless of the calendar.
  • Market shock triggerevent-basedAfter any single fund falls more than 20% in a short period, review the full portfolio to decide whether to rebalance or deploy reserves — do not wait for the annual review.
  • Major life changeevent-basedRebalance whenever a significant life event (job change, large expense, retirement nearing) alters your ability to tolerate market swings or your time horizon shortens materially.

4. Tactical Dip-Capture Playbook

  • Dip trigger thresholdthresholdDeploy money-market reserves into a falling equity fund when it has dropped 15% from its recent peak — this is the price-drop level (the dip trigger) that signals a meaningful buying opportunity for a Moderately Aggressive profile.
  • Staged deploymentstagedSplit the reserve into two tranches: deploy half at the 15% dip and the second half if the fund falls a further 10%, avoiding the risk of investing all reserves at a price that continues to fall.
  • Reserve floorreserveKeep at least 50% of the PeCDF-A money-market allocation intact at all times as an emergency buffer — never deploy the entire reserve into equities in a single move.
  • Recovery reloadOnce a dip-deployed position recovers past its prior peak, rebuild the money-market reserve over the following 6 months so the next dip can be captured with equal firepower.
