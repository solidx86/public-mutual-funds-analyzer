Before We Start — A Quick Foundation

You're new to unit trusts. Three short notes before we dive into the recommendations.

1. What is a Unit Trust?

A unit trust is a pool of money from many investors, professionally managed and invested across a basket of assets — stocks, bonds, cash — on your behalf. You own units; the fund manager does the work.

2. How Returns Work

Your investment grows in two ways: the price per unit (NAV) rises as the underlying assets appreciate, and some funds distribute income periodically. Reinvesting distributions compounds your growth.

3. Your Cooling-Off Right

As a first-time investor with Public Mutual, you have a 6 business day cooling-off period from the date of your first purchase. If you change your mind, you may redeem your units at the original NAV paid — no loss on the principal.

2. Distribution Policy

  • Reinvest distributionsreinvestAlways reinvest any distributions (income paid out by the fund) back into more units, so your money compounds — meaning growth builds on growth — over time.
  • No income withdrawalreinvestAvoid withdrawing distributions as cash; every ringgit taken out reduces the number of units growing for you, slowing progress toward your 9% annual return target.
  • Gold & money marketreinvestReinvest any income from PeEMAS and PeCDF-A too — these structural positions are designed to grow quietly in the background, not to pay you a regular income stream.

3. Rebalancing Triggers

  • Annual reviewtime-basedCheck the portfolio's allocation percentages once a year and top up or trim positions to bring them back to their target weights if they have drifted.
  • Drift banddrift-basedIf any single fund's allocation moves more than ±10% from its target weight, rebalance immediately — do not wait for the annual review date.
  • Macro event triggerevent-basedRebalance after a major market-moving event — such as a surprise US rate hike or a sharp ringgit move — that causes one fund to surge or fall significantly relative to the others.
  • Gold rebalancedrift-basedIf PeEMAS rises above 15% of the portfolio due to a gold rally, trim it back to 10.8% and redeploy the proceeds into the two core equity funds.

4. Tactical Dip-Capture Playbook

  • Dip triggerthresholdWhen PIATAF or PCSF falls 15% or more from its recent peak — known as the dip trigger (the price-drop level at which we move reserve money into the falling fund to buy units at a discount) — deploy reserves from PeCDF-A.
  • Staged deploymentstagedDo not move all reserves at once; split the deployment into two or three tranches spaced two to four weeks apart, so you average into the dip rather than catching a falling knife at a single price point.
  • Reserve floorreserveAlways keep at least 5% of the total portfolio in PeCDF-A as an undeployed buffer — never drain the money-market sleeve to zero, so you retain flexibility for a second dip.
  • Recovery top-upstagedAfter a dip-capture deployment, rebuild the PeCDF-A reserve over the following six to twelve months via your Regular Savings Plan (RSP — an automatic monthly investment, like a standing order) contributions, ready for the next opportunity.
